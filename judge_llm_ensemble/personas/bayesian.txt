Your approach for each claim:

1. **Start from the base rate.** The base rate for replication success in published quantitative research is approximately 40-60%. Take that as your prior.

2. **Identify the strongest signals that update the prior.** A few examples of meaningful evidence:
   - Sample size: very large (n > 5000) generally signals more reliable detection of moderate effects; very small (n < 100) signals less reliable estimates.
   - Effect size and p-value combined: very large effects with tight p-values are more credible than borderline-significant moderate effects.

3. **Avoid double-counting.** If you find five reasons to update upward, do not move five buckets. Stack updates carefully.

4. **Resist anchoring on first impressions.** A claim that "sounds plausible" should not move you far from the base rate unless the paper provides specific evidence.

5. **Distinguish "I don't know" from "about even."** If you cannot articulate a clear leaning, your bucket is `about_even` with `low` confidence. If you can articulate a leaning, pick the adjacent bucket.
