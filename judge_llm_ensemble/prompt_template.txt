You are rating the probability that a specific empirical claim from a published study reflects a **real effect** — i.e. would survive a well-powered direct replication in the same target population.

IMPORTANT — what you are NOT rating:
- You are NOT estimating whether a particular replication study has enough statistical power to detect the effect. Assume the replication is well-powered.
- You are NOT rating the importance, novelty, or theoretical interest of the claim.
- You are NOT rating whether the paper's other findings replicate. Only the supplied claim.

Output format (JSON, schema-validated):
1. `reasoning_top2`: two short sentences (<=25 words each) giving the two most important reasons behind your judgement. Write these FIRST, before picking the bucket — they should drive the bucket choice, not justify it after the fact.
2. `bucket`: one of the seven labels below.
3. `confidence_in_bucket`: `low` / `medium` / `high`.

The seven probability buckets:

  - `almost_certainly_not`   — <= 5%   (essentially no chance the effect is real)
  - `very_unlikely`          — 5-20%   (real-effect probability low but non-trivial)
  - `unlikely`               — 20-40%
  - `about_even`             — 40-60%  (genuine coin-flip; do not use as a default)
  - `likely`                 — 60-80%
  - `very_likely`            — 80-95%
  - `almost_certainly_yes`   — >= 95%  (effect is essentially certain to be real)

Calibration discipline:
- The middle bucket (`about_even`) is for genuine coin-flips, not "I'm not sure." If you can articulate a leaning, pick the adjacent bucket and set `confidence_in_bucket` accordingly.
- The two extreme buckets (<=5% / >=95%) require strong specific evidence — internal contradiction in the paper, or a claim so well-established it would be a stunning discovery if false. Do not put a typical "moderate-quality study" in these buckets.
